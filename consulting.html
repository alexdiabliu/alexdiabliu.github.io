Project Overview

Background & Objectives

The Rehabilitation Centre for Children (RCC) faces challenges delivering home exercise programs to families with limited English proficiency. Our objective was to design a solution that supports 5+ languages, maintains a < Grade 6 reading level, and reduces interpreter reliance.

Analysis & Methodology

We evaluated three options: a custom app (AccessPhysio), adopting Physitrack, and a Do-Nothing approach. Using a critical path analysis (shown below), we determined a 35-week implementation timeline was feasible.

Figure 1: The project critical path identified a 35-week timeline focused on PHIA compliance.

Recommendation

We recommend developing AccessPhysio. As seen in the Cash Flow Analysis below, while it requires upfront investment, it yields a positive Present Worth of $28,522 compared to Physitrack's negative return.

Course Reflection – IBEHS 4P03 Learning Portfolio

IBEHS 4P03 challenged me to move beyond purely technical problem-solving and think like a consultant working within a real health-care system. Our team’s project focused on improving access to pediatric physiotherapy instructions for families with limited English proficiency at the Rehabilitation Centre for Children (RCC). Throughout the term, I learned how engineering economics, project management, and stakeholder-centred design come together to support a solution that is not only technically feasible, but also financially justifiable and socially impactful.

My Role and Contributions

Within the team, I took a leading role in the scalability, strategy and project management components of the consulting report. I helped ideate methods to generate incoming cash flows for the proposed idea, ranging from external grants to licensing out the software to other clinics. I also aided in constrating the custom-built multilingual rehabilitation platform against adopting Physitrack and the do-nothing scenario in terms of feasibility and economies of scale, as it initially appeared as though Physitrack was more profitable. However this did not take into account the cheaper costs became as the platform scaled in size. Numbers such as these helped the team interpret what those numbers meant for RCC’s decision-making. I also contributed to developing the critical-path schedule, identifying which activities constrained the 35-week implementation timeline and where there was slack that could be used for crashing. Overall, my greatest contribution was bringing together the specializations of all team members into a cohesive executive summary as a foundation for the final report.

Beyond the quantitative work, I contributed to writing and refining the consulting narrative so that the recommendations were clear to non-technical readers. This meant translating concepts like benefit–cost trade-offs, economies of scale, and capital–labour substitutions into intuitive language that highlighted how the platform would actually change daily workflows for physiotherapists and families. Collaborating with my teammates on the design description and recommendations pushed me to think about how interface mockups, figures, and structured headings can reinforce a written argument rather than just decorate it.

Skills and Learning from the Course

One of the biggest skills I developed in this course was the ability to link economic tools to real design decisions. Before 4P03, concepts like present worth, MARR, and benefit–cost ratios felt abstract, a distant memory from previous courses. Working through our own spreadsheet, I saw how small changes in assumptions about interpreter usage or adherence rates could change whether a project looks attractive or risky. This directly reinforced lecture content on uncertainty, sensitivity analysis, and public decision-making, and helped me appreciate why assumptions must be clearly stated and justified rather than quietly “baked into” calculations.

I also gained experience in formal project management. Building the node diagram and Gantt-style schedule forced us to think carefully about dependencies, bottlenecks, and the difference between labour-intensive and capital-intensive phases of the project. Seeing how Activities A, B, D, E, F, G, H, L, and M formed the critical path made it clear that delays early in the requirements and prototyping stages would cascade into much longer deployment times. This has made me much more conscious of how I plan my own technical projects: getting alignment on scope early is not just “nice to have”, it has direct schedule and cost implications.

Alternative Outcomes and What I Would Do Differently

Looking back, there are a few key areas where our team’s decisions could have led to different outcomes. For example, we ultimately recommended the custom-built platform because it provided the strongest long-term present worth and the most flexibility for RCC’s needs. If we had placed more emphasis on short-term risk and implementation capacity, we might have leaned more heavily on the Physitrack adoption scenario as the primary recommendation, framing the custom platform as a longer-term “Phase 2” vision. That alternative would likely have reduced early development risk but at the cost of control over features, language expansion, and long-term equity goals.

On a personal level, I would also approach the modelling process differently next time. Early in the project, our team spent a lot of time refining the design narrative before fully locking in the economic assumptions. In future projects, I would push to build a rough economic model earlier and run simple sensitivity checks as we go. This would help us identify which parameters (for example, interpreter wage rates or expected changes in adherence) actually drive the decision, and where we need stronger justification or data. I also see opportunities to more actively integrate risk and uncertainty tools – such as scenario analysis or break-even plots – into our main argument rather than treating them as add-ons.

Impact on My Future Practice

Overall, IBEHS 4P03 has changed how I think about engineering work in health care. I now see economic evaluation, social benefit considerations, and project management not as separate boxes, but as interconnected lenses for judging whether a solution is truly viable. The project also showed me how strong communication – through writing, visuals, and structured web content like this portfolio – can help busy clinicians and stakeholders quickly understand why a recommendation matters. These skills will directly carry into my future design courses, capstone projects, and eventually into industry roles where engineering decisions must be justified to both technical and non-technical audiences.
